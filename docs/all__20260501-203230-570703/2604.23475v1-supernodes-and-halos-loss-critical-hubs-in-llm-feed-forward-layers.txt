# Abstract 

We study the organization of channel-level im-portance in transformer feed-forward networks (FFNs). Using a Fisher-style loss proxy (LP) based on activation-gradient second moments, we show that loss sensitivity is concentrated in a small set of channels within each layer. In Llama-3.1-8B, the top 1% of channels per layer accounts for a median of 58.7% of LP mass (range: 33.0– 86.1%). We call these loss-critical channels su-pernodes . Although FFN layers also contain strong activation outliers, LP-defined supernodes overlap only weakly with activation-defined out-liers and are not explained by activation power or weight norms alone. Around this core, we find a weaker but consistent halo structure: some non-supernode channels share the supernodes’ write support and show stronger redundancy with the protected core. We use one-shot structured FFN pruning as a diagnostic test of this organiza-tion. At 50% FFN sparsity, baselines that prune many supernodes degrade sharply, whereas our SCAR variants explicitly protect the supernode core; the strongest variant, S CAR -Prot, reaches perplexity 54.8 compared with 989.2 for Wanda-channel. The LP-concentration pattern appears across Mistral-7B, Llama-2-7B, and Qwen2-7B, remains visible in targeted Llama-3.1-70B exper-iments, and increases during OLMo-2-7B pre-training. These results suggest that LLM FFNs develop a small learned core of loss-critical chan-nels, and that preserving this core is important for reliable structured pruning.

1.1. Contributions 

Our contributions are fourfold. Together, they show that FFN importance is concentrated in a small loss-critical core and that preserving this core is often the difference between stable and unstable structured pruning. First , we define a Fisher-style channel loss proxy for FFN channels and use it to identify supernodes : the top 1% of channels by loss sen-sitivity. We show that these channels account for a large frac-tion of LP mass and that ablating them is far more damag-ing than ablating comparable non-supernode sets. Second ,we show that supernode hit-rate explains a major failure mode of one-shot structured FFN pruning. Methods that re-move more supernodes degrade more sharply, and controlled random-mask sweeps show a dose–response relationship between supernode removal and perplexity. Third , we char-acterize secondary halo structure around the supernode core. Write-halo channels share supernode write support and show higher redundancy with the protected core, while read-halo diagnostics suggest modest cross-layer dependence on the same support. Fourth , we validate LP concentration and core protection across Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B, Llama-2-7B, Qwen2-7B, targeted Llama-3.1-70B experiments, and OLMo-2-7B training checkpoints, where LP concentration increases during pretraining. 

# 2. Background and Related Work 

We first fix the channel-level notation used throughout the paper, then relate our work to three nearby lines of research: activation outliers, critical individual weights, and structured pruning. Supernodes sit between these prior phenomena. They are not individual weights, but they are also not generic activation outliers; they are structured FFN channels whose importance is defined through loss sensitivity.

2.2. Outlier Phenomena in LLMs 

Several recent results suggest that rare components can have disproportionate influence in LLM computation. These com-ponents have been studied at different granularities, from activation coordinates to individual weights. We review them because they motivate the central question of this pa-per: whether an analogous concentration exists at the level of whole FFN channels. 

Massive activations. Sun et al. (2024a) show that a small number of activation coordinates exhibit unusually large values that behave like implicit bias terms and can create at-tention sinks. We study FFN intermediate channels instead, defining supernodes by loss sensitivity rather than activation magnitude. 

Outliers in quantization. Activation outliers are also cen-tral to LLM quantization: LLM.int8() isolates outlier feature dimensions in higher precision (Dettmers et al., 2022), and SmoothQuant reduces activation outlier difficulty by shift-ing scale from activations to weights (Xiao et al., 2023). These works show that outlier structure matters for com-pression. They do not, however, identify a channel-level loss-critical FFN core or test its role in structured pruning. 

Super weights. Yu et al. (2024) identify individual weight parameters whose removal destroys model output. They show that as few as a single super weight can be critical, and that super weights induce corresponding super activations. In contrast, we focus on structured FFN channels spanning rows and columns across projections, identify them by loss sensitivity, and characterize their halo neighborhoods. Prior work shows that outlier activations and individual critical weights can strongly affect LLM behavior and com-pression. We extend this line of evidence to structured FFN channels. Specifically, we show that second-order channel sensitivity is concentrated in a small loss-critical core, that ablating this core is highly damaging, and that protecting it substantially improves one-shot structured FFN pruning. Halo structure provides an additional local signal for rank-ing non-core channels, but the primary contribution is the identification and validation of the supernode core. 

2.3. Structured Pruning of LLMs 

Pruning provides both a practical compression tool and a diagnostic for understanding which parts of a model are functionally important. In this paper, we use pruning in the second sense as well as the first: if a small set of channels is truly loss-critical, then pruning methods that remove those channels should fail more sharply than methods that protect them. Structured FFN pruning therefore gives a natural testbed for studying supernodes. 

Unstructured pruning. Wanda (Sun et al., 2024b) scores weights by |w|·| x| and achieves strong unstructured sparsity. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) uses second-order information for one-shot weight removal. These methods excel at fine-grained sparsity but do not yield hardware speedups without specialized sparse kernels. 3Loss-Critical Hubs in LLM FFNs 

Structured pruning. Group Fisher Pruning (Liu et al., 2021) uses Fisher information to score parameter groups for removal in CNNs, establishing that group-level Fisher sensitivity is an effective structured pruning criterion. LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023) performs structural pruning with LoRA recovery. OWL (Yin et al., 2024) extends Wanda with outlier-aware non-uniform layer-wise sparsity alloca-tion. OATS (Zhang & Papyan, 2025) decomposes weights into sparse + low-rank terms. The novelty here is not Fisher-based scoring itself, but the observation that such scoring reveals extreme concentration in a small channel subset (su-pernodes), and that protecting this subset accounts for much of the sharp pruning gap we observe. However, aggressive 

FFN channel removal (our focus) remains challenging in one-shot settings: at 50% channel sparsity, standard channel-adapted methods can suffer severe perplexity degradation. 

Why channel pruning is hard. We argue that part of the difficulty comes from supernode structure. The loss-critical core is so small that pruning rules not designed to protect it can remove many supernodes while still looking reasonable under aggregate saliency. Once a method removes even a modest fraction of this core, model quality can degrade sharply.

# 6. Validation: Structured Pruning as a Probe 

If supernodes capture genuine functional organization, prun-ing strategies that preserve them should be more robust than otherwise similar strategies that remove them. We use struc-tured FFN channel pruning to test this claim quantitatively, with halo features treated as a secondary refinement after the core is protected. 

6.1. Pruning Framework: S CAR 

We implement S CAR ( Supernode-Constrained A llocation for Resource-aware pruning), a one-shot structured pruning rule that excludes supernodes from the candidate prune set, ranks all remaining FFN channels by a chosen importance score, and selects channels under a global sparsity budget while enforcing per-layer sparsity caps. Halo-aware variants use redundancy and connectivity only after protecting the supernode core. 

Scoring variants. SCAR -LP prunes the lowest-LP i non-supernode channels and therefore tests the value of core protection alone. S CAR -Prot weights LP i by a redundancy-based protection score Protect i. S CAR -Conn further incor-porates connectivity, so that redundant halo channels are preferentially removed. The algorithm is detailed in Algorithm 1 (Appendix). Here, the pruning results serve mainly as evidence about the un-derlying structure. 

6.2. Experimental Setup 

We use the same one-shot pruning setup for all methods, so performance differences come from the pruning rule rather than recovery fine-tuning, extra data, or different evaluation conditions. 

Models. Llama-3.1-8B (primary, full suite), Mistral-7B, Llama-2-7B, Qwen2-7B (cross-model validation), plus Llama-3.1-70B for targeted scale validation (Touvron et al., 2023; Grattafiori et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). 

Calibration. Unless stated otherwise, we estimate LP ,

Conn , and contribution correlations from a calibration set of 64 sequences of length 512 drawn from the WikiText-2 training split (Merity et al., 2017) (and report sensitivity to calibration size/source, including C4 (Raffel et al., 2020)). 

Evaluation. Perplexity (WikiText-2, C4), zero-shot ac-curacy (HellaSwag, PIQA, BoolQ, WinoGrande, ARC-E, ARC-C, OpenBookQA), and few-shot accuracy (MMLU 5-shot) (Merity et al., 2017; Raffel et al., 2020; Zellers et al., 2019; Bisk et al., 2020; Clark et al., 2019; Sakaguchi et al., 2020; Clark et al., 2018; Mihaylov et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2021). 

Baselines. We compare against channel-adapted versions of Wanda, SparseGPT, OWL (Yin et al., 2024), LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023), FLAP (An et al., 2024), RIA (Zhang et al., 2024), and SlimLLM (Guo et al., 2025), as well as magnitude and random pruning. All methods are evaluated in the same one-shot setting without recovery fine-tuning. For methods originally designed for unstructured pruning, we aggregate per-weight saliencies over each FFN channel group: row i of Wup , row i of Wgate , and column i of Wdown .Thus, all methods remove the same object: whole SwiGLU FFN channels. 

SCAR Variants. We evaluate three SCAR scoring func-tions: SCAR-LP (raw loss proxy), SCAR-Prot (LP weighted by halo protection), and SCAR-Conn (LP weighted by connectivity-adjusted protection). All SCAR variants pro-tect the supernode core; S CAR -Prot and S CAR -Conn ad-ditionally use halo-derived signals to rank the remaining channels. This shared protocol makes the comparison direct: every method removes the same structured object, under the same sparsity budget, without post-pruning recovery.

6.4. Supernode Ablation: Direct Test of Core Removal 

The main comparison suggests that supernode hit-rate is a key variable. Here we test that more directly by changing the protected set and by exposing supernodes to pruning on purpose. We isolate this dependence through three con-trols: changing the protected set, forcing direct supernode removal, and comparing against random protection. 

Dependence on correct supernode identification. All SCAR variants depend on correctly identifying supernodes via the loss proxy (LP). S CAR -LP scores channels by LP alone, S CAR -Prot multiplies LP by a redundancy-based protection term, and S CAR -Conn additionally uses connec-tivity. At this sparsity, the key benefit of the LP-defined core is avoiding direct supernode hits; once that core is protected, fine distinctions among many non-core channels matter much less.

# 7. Cross-Model and Cross-Domain Consistency 

The analysis so far has centered on Llama-3.1-8B. We next test how much of the pattern carries over to other models, larger scale, training checkpoints, and downstream tasks. 

7.1. Cross-Model Validation 

LP concentration is not specific to Llama-3.1-8B, and the benefit of protecting high-sensitivity channels appears across several model families. The size of the pruning gap, however, varies by model. Across Mistral-7B, Llama-2-7B, and Qwen2-7B, LP remains sharply concentrated and SCAR-Conn achieves much lower perplexity than channel-adapted Wanda at 50% sparsity (Appendix Table A8); Fig-ure 3 shows the full sparsity–perplexity curves. We also observe LP concentration on OLMo-2-7B (Team OLMo et al., 2025), a fifth SwiGLU model from an independent training pipeline: at the final checkpoint the top 1% of FFN channels captures a median of 71.7% of LP mass (more concentrated than Llama-3.1-8B at 58.7%). OLMo also shows that pruning behavior can vary across model families: at 50% sparsity, SCAR-LP and channel-adapted Wanda are close in perplexity (15.43 vs. 15.20), while the clear ad-vantage over magnitude ( ∼134) and random ( ∼10 3) holds (Appendix Fig. A13). 

7.2. Emergence During OLMo-2 Pretraining 

A natural question is how supernodes arise. Pure factor decomposition (Appendix §D.4) isolates which statistical factor drives concentration, but not when it arises during training. Because OLMo-2-1124-7B provides dense inter-mediate checkpoints, we can track the loss proxy through pretraining on an architecturally matched SwiGLU model. Figure 5 follows three quantities from ∼1B to ∼4T training tokens. The median top-1% LP mass rises from 18.6% at the earliest checkpoint to 71.7% at the final model, with some non-monotonic variation mid-training, so concentration is acquired over training rather than already fully developed at the earliest available checkpoint. The LP max/mean ra-tio also increases overall, from 77 to 3690, although not monotonically. Excluding the final checkpoint’s 100% self-overlap, the per-layer Jaccard overlap with the final top-1% rises monotonically from 0.5% to 20.8%, indicating that the phenomenon stabilizes earlier than the specific channel identities do. The extended write-up, the final-checkpoint concentration table, and the OLMo pruning replication are provided in Appendix §E.

# 8. Limitations 

Several limitations remain. Supernode identification de-pends on calibration data, so large distribution shifts may require re-estimating the scores even if the phenomenon of extreme concentration still appears. We study FFN channels because the effect is clearest there; we do not test whether analogous loss-critical cores exist in attention blocks or MoE experts. LP is also a Fisher/Gauss–Newton approxi-mation and may miss nonlinear or non-Gaussian effects. There are also scope limitations. Although we include tar-geted 70B validation, the most complete benchmark cover-age is still at 7–8B scale. The OLMo checkpoint analysis is observational. It shows that LP concentration is acquired during pretraining, but it does not identify the training mech-anism that creates supernodes. Finally, we do not directly evaluate quantization, fine-tuning, or downstream model-editing interventions. These are natural applications of the supernode lens, but they are beyond what the present exper-iments establish.

# 9. Conclusion 

We identify a small loss-critical core within LLM FFN lay-ers. Under a Fisher-style activation-gradient proxy, channel-level sensitivity is highly concentrated, and direct ablations show that removing this core is far more damaging than removing matched non-core channels. In one-shot struc-tured FFN pruning, a major failure mode is direct supernode removal; protecting these channels explains much of the robustness gap between SCAR and several channel-pruning baselines. Halo structure provides a secondary ranking sig-nal around the protected core, but it is not the main driver of pruning failure. The same LP-concentration pattern appears across several model families, remains visible at 70B scale, and emerges during OLMo-2 pretraining. These results suggest that LLM FFNs develop a highly skewed channel-importance geometry, placing activation outliers and super weights alongside a structured channel-level counterpart that can be measured, protected, and potentially shaped during training. 

# Impact Statement 

This paper studies reliability in structured pruning for large language models. More reliable pruning can reduce memory use and computational cost, making models cheaper to run and easier to analyze. These efficiency gains may support beneficial uses, while also lowering barriers to misuse. Our contribution is analytical and methodological rather than application-specific; its broader impact depends on how improved compression methods are deployed.

D.4. LP-Defined vs Activation-Defined Supernodes 

A natural question is whether LP-defined supernodes differ from activation-defined ones, or whether they are mostly the same channels under another name.
