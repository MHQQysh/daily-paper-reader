## Attention at Rest Stays at Rest: Breaking Visual Inertia for Cognitive Hallucination Mitigation 

Boyang Gong, Yu Zheng †, Fanye Kong, Jie Zhou, and Jiwen Lu 

Tsinghua University, Beijing, China 

{gongby25, kongfy23}@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn, {yu-zheng, jzhou, lujiwen}@tsinghua.edu.cn 

Abstract. Like a body at rest that stays at rest, we find that visual attention in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) exhibits pro-nounced inertia, remaining largely static once settled during early de-coding steps and failing to support the compositional understanding re-quired for cognitive inference. While existing hallucination mitigation methods mainly target perceptual hallucinations concerning object ex-istence or attributes, they remain inadequate for such cognitive hallu-cinations that require inter-object relational deduction. Through token-wise attention analysis, we identify this visual inertia as a key fac-tor: attention to semantically critical regions remains persistently fo-cused and fails to dynamically support relational inference. We thereby propose a training-free Inertia-aware Visual Excitation (IVE) method that breaks this inertial pattern by modeling cognitive inference as the dynamic responsiveness of visual attention. Specifically, IVE selects vi-sual tokens that are dynamically emerging relative to historical attention trends while distinguishing tokens exhibiting inertial behavior. To fur-ther facilitate compositional inference, IVE introduces an inertia-aware penalty that discourages over-concentration and limits the persistence of attention within localized regions. Extensive experiments show that IVE is effective across various base MLLMs and multiple hallucination benchmarks, particularly for cognitive hallucinations. Code is available at https://github.com/wfr429/IVE .

have revealed that even advanced MLLMs may produce descriptions inconsis-tent with the provided images, a phenomenon known as MLLM hallucinations. This hallucination problem severely undermines MLLM reliability, limiting their practical applicability [3, 16, 29, 34, 46, 49]. To mitigate MLLM hallucinations, existing works can be broadly categorized into training-based and training-free methods. Training-based methods [20, 28, 39, 53, 54, 57] typically fine-tune models with instruction-response pairs or exter-nal supervision to enhance visual-linguistic alignment. In contrast, training-free methods [17,19,21,45,47,55,56,67] focus on improving visual grounding without parameter updates. However, these methods predominantly address perceptual hallucinations concerning object existence or basic attributes. As recent evalu-ations [12, 33] have shown, cognition represents a higher-order capability built upon perception and is critical for reliable deployment in scenarios requiring relational inference [3, 34, 49]. Cognitive hallucinations, where models fabricate inter-object relationships even after correctly recognizing individual objects (Fig-ure 1), are particularly difficult to detect through object-level verification, yet remain largely unaddressed by existing mitigation methods. To develop targeted solutions, it is necessary to first understand what causes cognitive hallucinations internally. Prior work on perceptual hallucinations has Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 3

traced their origins to the model’s attention over visual tokens, finding that insufficient attention to task-relevant regions leads to fabricated objects [32, 51, 55]. A natural question arises: do cognitive hallucinations share the same root cause? We argue that they do not, because cognitive inference requires not merely attending to individual objects, but dynamically integrating cues across multiple regions to capture inter-object relationships. This motivates our analysis of attention during cognitive tasks ( cf. Section 3), where we uncover a phenomenon we term Visual Inertia : as the model generates output, attention to key visual regions tends to remain persistently focused, failing to dynamically shift toward the compositional visual semantics required for relational inference. This finding also explains why naively amplifying visual attention [32] that is effective for perceptual hallucinations, yields diminishing returns for cognitive ones, as it reinforces static distributions rather than promoting the dynamic shifts that inference demands. To address this limitation, we propose a training-free Inertia-aware Visual Excitation (IVE) method to mitigate cognitive hallucinations. Unlike previous methods that simply amplify attention, IVE explicitly breaks the inertial pattern of visual attention, reactivating it to emphasize overlooked visual regions that require compositional understanding of inter-object relationships. Specifically, we first identify visual tokens that dynamically emerge relative to historical attention trends while separating tokens exhibiting inertial behavior, enabling the model to focus on newly relevant regions. To further prevent persistent over-concentration on localized areas, IVE applies an Inertia-aware Attention Penalty that adaptively penalizes attention to tokens with sustained inertia, promoting the capture of compositional visual semantics necessary for relational inference. Extensive experiments demonstrate that IVE is effective across various baseline MLLMs [2, 8, 30] and multiple hallucination benchmarks [12, 25, 33, 63], showing particularly strong performance on cognitive hallucination tasks. 

2 Related Works 

Multimodal Large Language Models: Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable breakthroughs in natural language under-standing and generation [1, 13, 27, 64]. Building on these advances, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), developed for multimodal understanding and generation, have evolved from BERT-based decoders [10] to LLM-based architectures [40, 42, 43, 65], which significantly improve multimodal perception and understanding [50, 52, 61, 62, 66]. For example, LLaVA [31] and LLaVA-1.5 [30] achieve strong multimodal joint understanding by integrating pre-trained visual encoders with text decoders and incorporating instruction fine-tuning. BLIP-2 [22] and InstructBLIP [8] introduce the Q-Former mechanism to ag-gregate multimodal features, which improves the accuracy of cross-modal in-teractions. Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct [2] further improves cross-modal alignment via modular connections between vision and language models. In this work, we use widely-adopted MLLM baselines including LLaVA-1.5 [30], InstructBLIP [8], 4 Boyang Gong, Yu Zheng †, Fanye Kong, Jie Zhou, and Jiwen Lu 

and Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct [2], to evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed hal-lucination mitigation method. 

Hallucination in MLLMs: Widely studied in LLMs [11,23,38,60], hallucina-tions refer to inconsistencies between generated content and real-world facts or user instructions. Similar issues have also been observed in MLLMs [40, 42, 43], where generated descriptions may include inaccurate object details or conflict with the input image. To quantify such phenomena, many studies have pro-posed benchmarks for hallucination evaluation. CHAIR [37] and POPE [25] re-spectively evaluate object hallucinations and object–attribute accuracy. While most existing benchmarks focus on such low-level hallucinations on primitive visual perception, recent studies have extended to cognitive-level aspects. Reef-knot [63] addresses the evaluation of these cognitive hallucinations involving complex inter-object relationships, whereas MME [12] and MMBench [33] fur-ther benchmark MLLMs at the reasoning level. We systematically evaluate the effectiveness of our method in mitigating hallucinations on these benchmarks. 

Hallucination Mitigation: Hallucination mitigation aims to make generated responses factually consistent with input text or visual content. In LLMs, re-searchers manipulate internal representations [23,24,59] to boost self-consistency across responses [36, 41]. For MLLMs, consistency requires non-conflicting se-mantics across modalities. Existing methods fall into two categories. Training-based approaches, like robust instruction tuning [18,28,53,57] and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [15, 20, 39, 54], use external supervision to reduce hallucinations at a high computational cost. Training-free methods mitigate perceptual hallucinations (object existence and attributes) by refining visual grounding during inference via early-layer features [17, 45] or perturbed inputs for contrastive decoding [4, 7, 21, 47]. While effective for perceptual-level hallucinations, cognitive-level hallucinations remain largely underexplored.

Recent studies [32, 51, 55] have iden-tified imbalanced token attention as a primary cause of perceptual hallu-cinations, where models fail to ade-quately attend to critical visual re-gions. Building on this finding, we conduct empirical analyses to investi-gate whether cognitive hallucinations exhibit similar attention patterns. Our analysis reveals that, while cognitive hallucinations also show to-ken attention imbalance, they exhibit an additional phenomenon referred to as Visual Inertia . To systematically assess it, we propose a metric that quantifies visual inertia. Attention to key visual regions exhibits pronounced visual inertia, gradually failing to dynami-cally shift toward the compositional semantics required for relational inference as generation progresses. This renders existing attention amplification methods [32] less effective for cognitive hallucinations. 

Limited Effectiveness of Attention Amplification: To investigate whether amplifying attention to visual tokens Tv mitigates cognitive hallucinations, we progressively increased the amplification factor following PAI [32]. As shown in Figure 2, mitigation of perceptual hallucinations on POPE [25] improves mono-tonically with stronger amplification. In contrast, cognitive hallucinations on Reefknot [63] show a non-monotonic pattern: performance first improves but declines when amplification is excessive. The observed performance degradation indicates that attention insufficiency alone does not fully explain cognitive hal-lucinations, and naive amplification fails to address the underlying cause.

4 Approach 

In this section, we present the proposed Inertia-aware Visual Excitation (IVE) framework, as illustrated in Figure 4. Rather than heuristically manipulating attention weights, IVE is grounded in the insight that cognitive inference ca-pability is closely related to the temporal dynamics of visual attention across decoding steps relative to historical attention trends, which requires breaking vi-sual attention inertia to enable compositional understanding. We first introduce Trend-guided Token Selection, which identifies visual tokens that dynamically emerge relative to historical attention trends while separating tokens exhibiting inertial behavior, followed by Inertia-aware Attention Penalty, which penalizes persistent over-concentration on localized regions.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose Inertia-aware Visual Excitation (IVE) method to mit-igate cognitive hallucinations. Unlike existing methods that mainly target per-ceptual hallucinations concerning object existence or attributes, IVE explicitly models cognitive inference as the dynamic responsiveness of visual attention. In-spired by the observation that attention to visual tokens often exhibits persistent inertia, IVE identifies visual tokens that emerge relative to historical attention trends while distinguishing tokens exhibiting inertial behavior, enabling adap-Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 15 

tive attention reallocation. To further mitigate persistent attention on inertia tokens and promote compositional inference, IVE introduces an inertia-aware penalty that discourages over-concentration and constrains persistent attention within localized regions. Extensive experiments demonstrate that IVE is effec-tive across various base MLLMs and multiple hallucination benchmarks, with particular success in addressing cognitive hallucinations. 16 Boyang Gong, Yu Zheng †, Fanye Kong, Jie Zhou, and Jiwen Lu
