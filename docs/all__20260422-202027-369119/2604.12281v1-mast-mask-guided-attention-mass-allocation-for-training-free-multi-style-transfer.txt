# MAST: Mask-Guided Attention Mass Allocation for Training-Free Multi-Style Transfer 

## Dongkyung Kang *, Jaeyeon Hwang *, Junseo Park *, Minji Kang *, Yeryeong Lee *, Beomseok Ko, Hanyoung Roh, Jeongmin Shin, Hyeryung Jang †

Division of Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence, Dongguk University Republic of Korea 

Abstract 

Style transfer aims to render a content image with the visual charac-teristics of a reference style while preserving its underlying seman-tic layout and structural geometry. While recent diffusion-based models demonstrate strong stylization capabilities by leveraging powerful generative priors and controllable internal representa-tions, they typically assume a single global style. Extending them to multi-style scenarios often leads to boundary artifacts, unsta-ble stylization, and structural inconsistency due to interference between multiple style representations. To overcome these limi-tations, we propose MAST (Mask-Guided Attention Mass Alloca-tion for Training-Free Multi-Style Transfer), a novel training-free framework that explicitly controls content-style interactions within the diffusion attention mechanism. To achieve artifact-free and structure-preserving stylization, MAST integrates four connected modules. First, Layout-preserving Query Anchoring prevents global layout collapse by firmly anchoring the semantic structure using content queries. Second, Logit-level Attention Mass Allocation de-terministically distributes attention probability mass across spatial regions, seamlessly fusing multiple styles without boundary ar-tifacts. Third, Sharpness-aware Temperature Scaling restores the attention sharpness degraded by multi-style expansion. Finally, Discrepancy-aware Detail Injection adaptively compensates for lo-calized high-frequency detail losses by measuring structural discrep-ancies. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MAST effectively mitigates boundary artifacts and maintains structural consistency, preserving texture fidelity and spatial coherence even as the number of applied styles increases. 

1 Introduction 

Image style transfer aims to render a content image with the visual characteristics of a reference style while preserving its underlying semantic layout and structural geometry. This task has long been a core challenge in computer vision and graphics, with applica-tions ranging from artistic image generation to creative produc-tion [ 7, 15 , 17 ]. Early methods [ 9, 32 ] primarily relied on CNN-based optimization or global statistical matching. To improve efficiency and capture long-range dependencies, subsequent works [ 6 , 23 , 33 ]adopted GAN and Transformer architectures. Despite their progress, these non-diffusion approaches often rely on global feature statis-tics, which fail to capture fine-grained local structures and fre-quently lead to shape distortions or texture leakage across object boundaries.

To overcome these limitations, diffusion-based models [ 5, 8 , 29 ]have emerged as a superior paradigm, leveraging strong gener-ative priors and controllable internal representations to achieve high-fidelity stylization. However, most existing diffusion frame-works [ 5, 11 , 25 , 30 ] are inherently designed for single-style transfer, assuming a uniform global style for the entire image. As the demand for more granular control grows, such as applying distinct stylistic intentions to specific objects within a single scene, extending these models to multi-style scenarios becomes essential. The primary technical obstacle in multi-style expansion is the interference between multiple style representations within the dif-fusion attention mechanism. Since the internal attention process is inherently global, style information intended for one masked region via naive pixel-level masking often leaks into unintended areas , causing unwanted blending and visual noise. Furthermore, as the model attempts to incorporate multiple style references simul-taneously, its focus becomes weakened - a phenomenon we define as “attention flattening” . This dilution of stylistic focus prevents the model from capturing the vivid textures of each intended style,

Unlike previ-ous methods that struggle to block internal style leakage, MAST 

achieves precise multi-style fusion at the logit level through the following technical pillars: 

• Logit-level Attention Mass Allocation ( LAMA ): To eliminate style conflict, LAMA deterministically rescales the distribution of stylistic resources, i.e., attention probability mass, at the net-work’s logit level. By satisfying the probability-mass conserva-tion principle, it ensures that each style is injected precisely into its designated region, preventing leakage and ensuring artifact-free transitions. 

• Sharpness-aware Temperature Scaling ( STS ): To counteract atten-tion flattening, STS acts as an active focus recovery mechanism. It adaptively restores the model’s confidence by sharpening the internal attention distribution, enabling the vivid rendering of distinct stylistic textures even in complex multi-style scenarios. 

• Dual-scale structural preservation ( LQA and DDI ): We employ a two-tiered strategy to protect content integrity at both global and local scales. Layout-preserving Query Anchoring ( LQA ) acts as a global anchor to stabilize the overall semantic structure, while Discrepancy-aware Detail Injection ( DDI ) restores fine tex-tures and sharp edges by selectively compensating for structural discrepancies identified in the frequency domain. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MAST produces state-of-the-art results, maintaining high texture fidelity and spatial co-herence even as the number of applied styles increases. In summary, our key contributions are: (i) a unified training-free framework for multi-style transfer; (ii) a deterministic logit-level style allocation via LAMA to prevent inter-style interference; (iii) the STS mecha-nism for restoring stylistic clarity and focus; and (iv) a dual-scale content preservation strategy, i.e., LQA and DDI , to robustly main-tain structural fidelity against strong stylistic injections. 

2 Related work 

Image Style Transfer. Early CNN-based methods [ 9] utilized per-ceptual features and global statistics, often failing to capture fine-grained style details or achieve proper disentanglement. Subsequent GAN- [ 23 , 33 ] and Transformer-based [ 6, 19 , 24 ] approaches im-proved efficiency and long-range dependency modeling. They en-able flexible and precise stylization with real-time and multi-modal control, as well as complex semantics. However, these methods frequently struggle with structural preservation and precise style control, motivating the rise of diffusion-based alternatives as a more stable paradigm. 

Diffusion-based Style Transfer. Diffusion models offer superior stylization quality, categorized into training-based and training-free approaches. Training-based methods [ 2, 3 , 10 , 25 , 30 ] fine-tune models on content–style pairs to improve consistency and con-trollability. In contrast, training-free methods manipulate internal representations - such as attention maps or latent features - to enable flexible stylization without additional training [ 4, 5, 12 , 14 ,15 , 26 , 28 , 31 ]. Image-guided techniques refine the process through attention reweighting, e.g., StyleID [ 4] and 𝑍 ∗ [5], or content-aware normalization [ 12 , 14 , 28 ], while prompt-driven methods guide styl-ization via cross-model alignment or textual inversion [ 15 , 26 , 31 ]. Despite their flexibility, existing diffusion-based methods are primarily designed for single-style transfer and lack explicit mecha-nisms for handling multiple styles across spatial regions. This often results in interference and artifacts when multiple styles are intro-duced. Our approach is a training-free framework that explicitly addresses mask-based multi-style transfer through deterministic attention mass allocation.

Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate style transfer in terms of both content preservation and style fidelity using complementary met-rics: FID, LPIPS, LPIPS-Gray, CFSD, and their combined metric, ArtFID. ArtFID is used as the primary metric, as it jointly captures content and style quality and correlates well with human percep-tion: ArtFID = (1 + LPIPS ) · ( 1 + FID ). FID measures the distribu-tional similarity between generated and style images, reflecting style fidelity. LPIPS evaluates perceptual similarity to the content image, measuring content preservation. LPIPS-Gray removes color information to focus on structural consistency. CFSD measures structural consistency via spatial feature correlations, reducing sensitivity to style-induced textures. Additionally, for mask-based multi-style transfer, we introduce Masked FID (M-FID) to evaluate stylization within specific regions. We restrict feature aggregation to masked areas and compute FID only over the stylized regions. 

Implementation details. All experiments are conducted using Stable Diffusion v1.4 with DDIM sampling (50 steps). We set 𝜆 =

0.2, 𝜋 ∗ = 0.9 and 𝑟 = 0.3. Our method operates on self-attention modules in the U-Net, applying feature manipulation at all timesteps to decoder layers 7–12, where high-level semantic interactions are dominant. Text prompts are set to null strings.

5.2 Multi-style Scalability 

Our method demonstrates strong scalability, maintaining consistent performance and stable style composition as the number of styles increases. To qualitatively validate this, Fig. 7 presents results under a 5-style mask setting with sequential style addition. Even with narrow mask regions and increasing style complexity, styles are consistently applied across content and background while preserv-ing object identity. Previously applied styles remain stable as new styles are introduced, with clear separation between foreground and background and no noticeable style leakage. For example, even with strong style images (last two rows, with target spatial masks shown in orange and blue), no style inter-ference is observed in adjacent content regions (e.g., the dog and pizza). At the same time, the distinctive visual characteristics of each style—such as the lighting in the ice castle and the vivid colors of the cat—are faithfully preserved. For quantitative evaluation, we measure ArtFID while increasing the number of styles from 1 to 5, as shown in Fig. 8. Most baseline methods suffer significant performance degradation when transi-tioning from single-style to multi-style settings, with a sharp drop at the 1→2 transition, as reflected in the violin plots, since they are not explicitly designed for mask-based multi-style transfer. In contrast, our method improves performance from 1→2 and remains stable thereafter, demonstrating effective style disentan-glement and control. While methods such as 𝑍 ∗ and StyleID show relatively stable trends, they still underperform compared to ours. Quantitatively, our method achieves the lowest MAD across vary-ing numbers of styles, indicating strong robustness as the number of styles increases. 

5.3 Analysis of Multi-Style Interaction 

In multi-style settings, unlike naive copy-and-paste approaches (see Fig. 1), styles are not applied independently but interact within the overall composition. In particular, changes in the background style directly affect the appearance of the character style in the 2-style setting. As shown in Fig. 9, each row keeps the same char-acter style while varying only the background style. Even with an identical character style, its visual expression changes depending on the accompanying background, indicating that style appearance is determined by the global composition. This behavior arises from our multi-style design and can be ex-plained by the combined effects of LAMA and STS . LAMA processes multiple styles jointly with respect to a shared content anchor, rather than independently. Each style adjusts its relative influence in relation to others, so changes in one style affect the overall com-positional balance, leading to coherent integration across styles. Moreover, different style combinations produce different atten-tion logit distributions, resulting in variations in log 𝑝 max . Since 𝜏 is determined based on log 𝑝 max , each combination yields a different temperature. Consequently, STS adaptively modulates the sharp-ness of the attention distribution, causing even the same style to exhibit different visual characteristics. Overall, our method enables adaptive variation in style expres-sion while maintaining balanced integration, resulting in coherent multi-style compositions. This explains the strong scalability ob-served in Sec. 5.2.

A.4 Additional Multi-Style Results 

A.4.1 User study. We conducted a user study comparing our method with all baselines. Specifically, we prepared 99 stylized results using 

1–4 style images, and 30 participants evaluated each result. The evaluation was based on six criteria: style faithfulness, content preservation, boundary naturalness, structural consistency, detail preservation, and overall visual quality. The results are summarized in Fig. 12. Fig. 12a shows the per-method results in the single-style setting, while Fig. 12b presents those in the multi-style setting with 2–4 styles. Our method receives consistently stronger ratings than the baselines in both settings. Although some methods, such as StyleID [ 4] and CAST [ 32 ], achieve competitive quality in the single-style setting, their scores tend to drop when extended to the multi-style setting. In contrast, our method performs even better in the multi-style setting, indicating that the proposed design effectively balances and harmonizes style and content, leading to more satisfactory results. 

A.4.2 Scalability Across Multi-Style Settings. Table 6 summarizes the quantitative results from 1-style to 5-style settings. Our method consistently achieves the best or near-best performance on ArtFID, FID, and CFSD, showing that it maintains both stylization quality and structural consistency as the number of styles increases. Al-though some baselines obtain lower LPIPS in certain cases, this often reflects weaker stylization or a stronger bias toward content preservation rather than better overall transfer quality. Overall, our method achieves a better balance between style fidelity and structural preservation across all style counts. Fig. 14 provides qualitative comparisons with baselines from 1-style to 4-style settings. We exclude the 5-style case here because, at this level, each mask region becomes too small relative to the image resolution, making the visual comparison less informative. Even under increasing style complexity, existing methods often suffer from insufficient style transfer, inconsistent textures, or boundary artifacts. In contrast, our method produces more coherent styliza-tion while preserving object structure and generating smoother transitions across regions. Fig. 15 further demonstrates the robustness of our method under diverse multi-style settings. As our framework is designed for multi-style transfer, it remains effective across different numbers of styles as well as varying aspect ratios and image resolutions from 512 to 

1024 . These results show that our method generalizes well beyond a fixed setup and maintains stable region-wise style injection under more challenging spatial configurations. 

A.4.3 Style Fidelity Comparison in the 2-Style Setting. Fig. 16 presents a comparison between our method and strong baselines ( 𝑍 ∗ [ 5], StyleID [ 4 ], and StyleShot [ 8]) in the 2-style setting. To highlight style fidelity, we additionally provide zoomed-in views of the refer-ence style images and the corresponding stylized regions. These comparisons show whether characteristic style attributes, such as texture patterns and color tones, are faithfully transferred to the target regions. While the baselines produce competitive results, they often fail to fully capture the local stylistic characteristics of the reference images, resulting in weakened textures, inaccurate color appear-ance, or less distinctive stylization. In contrast, our method more faithfully preserves the target style attributes in each region, yield-ing stylizations that are both visually coherent and more consistent with the reference styles.
